Introduction to Pad Printing

What is Pad Printing?

Pad printing (also reffered to as tampography) is a manufacturing printing process that transforms a 2-dimensional image onto a 3-dimensional body by actuating an applicator between two offsets. The most common industries include: medical, automotive, apparel, and electronics!

Cycle Overview

  • I: Home Position
    • The ink cup (inverted to trap the ink) rests over an etched out image on the printing plate (clich√©).
  • II: Leave Home Position
    • The ink cup is linearly actuated away from the ink-filled etched image. The ink becomes tacky due to the exposure to air which aids its adhesive properties.
  • III: Actuate Transfer Pad Downward
    • The transfer pad is linearly actuated downward onto the etching momentarily where the ink adheres to the pad surface.
  • IV: Actuate Transfer Pad Upward
    • The transfer pad is linearly actuated upward. A negligible amount of ink is left in the ethcing.
  • V: Actuate Transfer Pad and Ink Cup
    • As the transfer pad is actuated over the substrate, the ink cup is actuated back over the etching to prepare for the next cycle.
  • VI: Actuate Transfer Pad Downward
    • The transfer pad is actuated downward onto the subtrate such that the pressure is great enough to completely transfer the image and conform to the substrate's geometry.

Dual Pad Printing

At Cadence, pad printing is optimized by using the PS-2200 2-color pad printer in order to keep up with demand. This is necessary because the Stryker Torque Wrench requires a mirrored image on the other half of the external sleeve. With the addition of dual sided pad printing, labor is essentially cut in half since the operator does not have to print one side at a time.

Tangible Enhancements: Problems

Wrench Deflection During Printing

One of the immediate irregularities that was discovered was the deflection of the torque wrench when pressure was applied. Not only does this add additional compressive and tensiles stresses to the external housing, it retards the ability of the applicator to print the Stryker logo. As can be seen in the short video, the applicator applies a significant distributed load such that a fixture needed to be built. This fixture is supposed to support the handles of the wrench; however, it failed to do so. In order to stop the applicator from bending the wrench, a step was added to the fixture to secure a better contact surface on the wrench.

Although this cannot be seen in the video, the fixture's geometry is designed such that the wrench's base sits on a concentric cylinder. This base support is counteracted by a pin at the head of the wrench which results in a sufficient reaction force and allows the wrench to "snap" in place.

Shifted Print (Double Vision)

As can be seen, double vision is derived from pad printed parts that have shifted images. This occurs when the operator needs to clean the applicator. Often times, an applicator needs to be cleaned because they attract particles and/or an excessive amount of ink has built up on the surface. Both of these issues typically produce incomplete images on the wrench's body which requires another cycle of clean printing.

In order to properly clean the applicator, the operator needs to remove the upper fixture that the applicator slides into. Once the operator cleans the applicator using the TPV thinner, the applicator gets mounted back onto the fixture. The issue is that there is no way to lock the applicator in the same position every time; therefore, the second cycle of pad printing causes produces the defect known as double vision.

Other notable issues with the printing process revolve around the viscosity of the mixture. If the mixture is too viscous, then the print will tend to miss spots as opposed to a less viscous mixture which will tend to smear/run.

Tangible Enhancements: Solutions

Wrench Deflection During Printing

The solution to fix wrench deflection was quite simple. In order to get a better contact between the fixture and the external housing arms, a shim was added to the base of the fixture to raise it up. This simple fix comfortably allows the applicator to apply an evenly distributed load on the external housing.

As can be seen in the video, the part is secured properly and thus subject to less stress. More importantly, the prints are more clear towards the head of the housing.

Shifted Print (Double Vision)

The solution to eliminate the ongoing issue of double vision is to design and attach a mount. As can be seen in the animation, the applicator assembly (magenta applicator, wood base plate, and purple adapter) is freely allowed to move linearly within the upper fixture (teal plate).

With the addition of a mount/stopper (red plate), the applicator assembly with catch on the front face of the upper fixture. The width of the mount/stopper is dependent on the desired distance of the operator.

Procedural Enhancements: Mixture Analysis

Measuring Procedure

Ink

  • Tare the scale with the paper measuring cup
    • Taring the scale with the cup would be optimal
  • Add the required amount of TPR ink
  • Pour all of the measured ink into the cup

Thinner

  • Again, tare the scale with a paper measuring cup
  • Based on the amount of ink previosuly added, add the respective amount of TPV thinner into the paper cup
  • Pour all of the measured thinner into the ink-filled cup and mix

Typical Measurements

Full Working Day

  • Typically, on a full working day where the operator is pad printing for ~7 hours, 71g of ink and 4g of thinner is ideal. The goal is to have just enough mixture at the end of the shift to keep the machine functioning properly.

Half Working Day

  • Opposed to cutting the full working day measurements in half, the operator should likely stay in the range of 62g ink and 3.5g thinner. This relatively large amount of mixture is necessary to keep the etching filled towards the end of the shift
